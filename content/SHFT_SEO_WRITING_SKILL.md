# SHFT SEO Writing Skill
## The standard for every blog post and hub page we write for clients.

This document defines how SHFT writes SEO content. It covers structure, voice, formatting, keyword use, and the rules that separate a SHFT post from a generic AI draft. Read it fully before writing any post. Apply every rule unless the client's tone of voice guide explicitly overrides it.

---

## 01 Post Types

Every piece of content is either a **hub page** or a **spoke post**. Know which one you're writing before you start.

### Hub pages
Hub pages are the five pillar topics that anchor a client's SEO strategy. They are comprehensive, definitional, and internally linked to all spoke posts in their cluster.

- Target: one high-volume primary keyword for the hub topic
- Length: 2,500–4,000 words
- Required closing section: **FAQs** (minimum 5 questions, targeting long-tail and People Also Ask queries)
- Required: links to every spoke post in its cluster
- Internal links: at least two links to BOFU service or contact pages

### Spoke posts
Spoke posts are the five blog articles under each hub. They go deeper on a specific sub-topic within the hub's theme.

- Target: one MOFU primary keyword with 2–4 secondary keywords
- Length: 2,000–3,000 words
- Closing section: **Relevant Resources** list (3–5 internal or external links that help the reader go further), if applicable
- Required: one link back to its parent hub page
- Internal links: at least one link to a BOFU service or contact page

## 02 Metadata Format

Every post is delivered with this metadata block at the top of the document. Never skip any field.

| Field | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Type** | Hub or Spoke |
| **SEO Title (title tag)** | Primary keyword front-loaded. 50–60 characters. Includes brand name suffix if needed (e.g., "\| SHFT Agency"). |
| **On-Page Title (H1)** | Can be slightly different from the SEO title — more conversational, no pixel limit. |
| **Subtitle** | One punchy sentence. States the core promise of the post. |
| **Meta Description** | Under 160 characters. Includes primary keyword. Written to earn the click — not just describe the post. |
| **URL Slug** | Lowercase, hyphens only. Includes primary keyword. No stop words unless essential. |
| **Primary Keyword** | The single keyword this page owns. |
| **Secondary Keywords** | 2–4 supporting keywords with volume/KD noted. Each should appear naturally in at least one H2 or H3. |

## 03 Intro Structure

Every post opens the same way. No exceptions.

**1. Open with a bold problem statement.**
One sentence. Something the reader immediately recognizes as their reality. No preamble, no "In today's world," no warm-up.

**2. Paint the situation in 2–3 short sentences.**
Use fragments. Drop the verb from the second sentence if it creates rhythm. "The founder says one thing. Sales another. And the website sounds like every single competitor."

**3. Name the problem explicitly.**
One diagnostic sentence. "When your team doesn't know why customers should buy from you, you have a positioning problem." Don't jump straight to the fix — name it first.

**4. Deliver the fix in one sentence.**
Use a question-answer format when possible. "The solution? Write a positioning statement." Or state it flat: "That's where IIoT changes the game."

**5. Define the concept briefly.**
One sentence. Don't wait for the first H2.

**6. State the key question the post answers** — in italics, as its own line.
*Why should customers choose us over our competitors?*

**7. Connect to revenue within the next two sentences.**
Don't wait until paragraph three. What does solving this problem actually mean for the business?

**8. State what the post covers.**
Specific. "This post will show you what a positioning statement is, why you need one, and how to write yours — including the exact framework and examples from real clients." Not vague: "This guide walks you through how to do a brand audit."

**9. End with: "Let's dive in."**
Not "Let's get into it." Not "Let's go." Always "Let's dive in."

**Critical intro rule:** Assume the reader has nothing. No strategy deck, no existing document, no formal framework. The pain is "I can't explain what makes us different" — not "our document is gathering dust."

## 04 Header Hierarchy and Keyword Rules

**H2s** — Major section breaks. Title case. Use question format for concept-definition sections ("What Is a Positioning Statement?"). Secondary keywords should appear naturally in at least two H2s across the post.

**H3s** — Subsection headers. Sentence case, not title case. Short and direct. "Why most fail" not "Why Most Strategies Fall Flat and What to Do About It."

**H4s** — Use only when a subsection needs further breakdown. Not required.

**Keyword placement rules:**
- Primary keyword: appears in the H1, the intro paragraph, at least two H2s, and the conclusion
- Secondary keywords: appear in H3s and body copy where they fit naturally
- Never force a keyword if it sounds unnatural — rewrite the sentence first

---

## 05 Paragraph and Sentence Rules

These apply regardless of client. They are the SHFT writing standard.

- **1–3 sentences per paragraph.** Single-sentence paragraphs are allowed and often preferred for emphasis.
- **Front-load the point.** Start each paragraph with the claim or conclusion. Support it after.
- **One idea per paragraph.** If the topic shifts, start a new one.
- **Use sentence fragments for rhythm.** "New campaigns. More content. Additional offers." Creates pace. Better than elaborate scenario-painting.
- **Use incomplete sentence pairs.** "The founder says one thing. Sales another." Drop the verb from the second sentence. Trust the reader.
- **Let strong statements land.** Don't follow a punchy line with 2–3 sentences explaining why it's true. Say it. Move on.
- **Use "Period." as a standalone emphasis word.** End a paragraph with it to close a point with finality: "If your team can't repeat your positioning statement from memory, it will never get implemented. Period."
- **Use italics for the one key question the post answers.** Set it apart as its own paragraph.

## 06 Bullet and List Styles

Use the right style for the context. Mixing them up is an AI tell.

**Style A — Clean bullets (no bold)**
For lists of outcomes, stats, benefits, or overview items. The reader is scanning. No lead phrases, no explanations.
- 58% higher revenue growth
- 73% higher customer loyalty
- 36% more repeat purchases

**Style B — Bold lead phrase + explanation**
For instructional lists, steps, attributes to uncover, or anything where each item needs context.
- **Desired outcome.** What are they hoping to achieve with an offer like yours?
- **Desired experience.** How do they want to engage with a company like yours?

Also use Style B for implementation steps and "ways to" lists.
- **Update your offers.** Make sure your services live up to the perception and claims in your positioning statement.
- **Use it as a decision filter.** New offers, partnerships, and campaigns should all pass through the lens of your positioning.

**Style C — Plain checklist (no formatting)**
For lists of things to investigate, map out, or check. Items are self-explanatory.
- Category
- Strengths
- Weaknesses
- Differentiated claims

## 07 Client Examples and Proof

Every major claim needs proof. Use them in this priority order:
1. Verified client result with specifics ("one client achieved a 100% close rate")
2. Named framework with attribution ("Marty Neumeier's Onlyness Statement")
3. Specific stat with source
4. Qualified range when verified data isn't available ("a rise of 30–50% isn't unusual")

**How to use client examples:**
- Introduce the example with context: "We saw this play out with [Client]."
- Tell the before state, what changed, and the result.
- Keep it concise — one to three paragraphs max per story.
- Three examples is enough. Depth beats quantity. Don't list six clients with one sentence each.
- Explain why the example matters to the point being made.

**Image suggestion format:**
Use bracketed callouts when recommending visuals. These are not written in the post copy — they appear as editor notes.

> **IMAGE SUGGESTION**
> *What the image should show — specific, not generic.*
> Alt text: "Descriptive alt text using the target keyword where natural."

## 08 Section Ordering

Establish the concept and its revenue impact before critiquing common failures. The reader needs to understand what good looks like before they can recognize what bad looks like.

Correct order:
1. What is it / define the concept
2. Why it matters / the revenue connection
3. Why most approaches fail
4. How to do it right (step-by-step or framework)
5. How to make it stick / implement it
6. Closing section (FAQs or Resources) + CTA

**Framework placement:** If a post has a proprietary framework or template, introduce it the first time it's relevant — not when you reach the step-by-step section. Show the reader the shape of the thing early. The how-to section fills in details of something they've already seen.

---

## 09 Closing Sections

**Hub pages — FAQs**
Minimum 5 questions. Target long-tail queries and People Also Ask boxes. Format:

**Q: [Question]**
[Answer — 2–4 sentences. Direct. No fluff.]

**Spoke pages — Relevant Resources** (if applicable)
A short list of 3–5 links (internal and external) that help the reader go further. Brief description of each.

**Related Articles:**
- [Link text](URL) — One sentence on what it covers and why it's relevant.

**Every post — CTA**
Always close with a specific call to action. Link to a service page, contact form, or lead magnet. Make it clear what happens when they click. Don't end a post without a next step.

## 10 Word Choice Rules

These are non-negotiable SHFT vocabulary standards. They apply across every client.

| Use This | Never Use This |
|---|---|
| ideal customers | target audience |
| one-liner | tagline |
| purpose statement | mission statement |
| offer / offers | product/service (when speaking broadly) |
| the competition | the next vendor |
| B2B service companies | B2B companies (always include "service") |
| use, apply, build on | leverage (as a verb) |
| what actually works | best practices |
| system, framework, process | solutions |
| dig into, break down, unpack | deep dive |
| real, honest, consistent | authentic / authenticity |
| strong, thorough | robust |
| complete, end-to-end | holistic |
| content, education, expertise | thought leadership |
| alignment, connection | synergy |
| follow up, revisit | circle back |

**Punctuation standards:**
- Contractions always: it's, don't, you're, that's, won't
- Em dashes for contrast and dramatic pauses — one per paragraph is usually enough
- Semicolons for connected ideas
- Parenthetical asides for clarification and personality (not formal explanations)
- Oxford comma, consistent throughout
- Single quotes for words being discussed as words: "Statements like 'innovative' or 'client-centric' describe every business."
- Curly braces for template variables: {category}, {unique value}

## 11 What to Avoid — The Anti-AI Checklist

Before submitting any draft, check for these patterns. They are the most common AI tells.

**Banned words and phrases — never use:**

*Overused AI filler words:*
- additionally, furthermore, moreover — just start the next sentence
- crucial, vital, key (as an adjective meaning "important") — say why it matters instead
- delve, delve into — use "dig into," "break down," "unpack"
- enhance — use "improve," "strengthen," or be specific about what changes
- ensure — usually cuts cleanly; rewrite the sentence
- foster — use "build," "create," "develop"
- garner — use "earn," "get," "attract"
- highlight (as a verb) — use "show," "point to," "reveal"
- interplay — almost always vague; be specific about the relationship
- intricate / intricacies — use "complex" or describe it directly
- landscape (abstract noun) — "the marketing landscape," "the regulatory landscape" — just name the thing
- pivotal — use "critical," "turning point," or be specific
- profound — cut it or say what makes it significant
- renowned — use "well-known" or name the specific reputation
- showcase — use "show," "demonstrate"
- tapestry (figurative) — always cut
- testament — "a testament to their work" → "proof of their work"
- underscore (as a verb) — use "reinforce," "show," "point to"
- valuable — say why it has value or cut it
- vibrant — cut it; describe what's actually happening
- nestled — cut it
- "in the heart of" — cut it
- groundbreaking (figurative) — say what's actually new about it
- rich (figurative, as in "a rich history") — be specific
- align with / aligns with — use "matches," "fits," "supports"
- emphasizing — restructure the sentence

*Weak construction words:*
- "serves as" / "stands as" — just use "is"
- "boasts," "features," "offers" when "has" is more direct and natural
- "not just X, but Y" / "not only X, but also Y" — negative parallelism; cut the "not just" and make a direct claim

*Banned phrases:*
- "In today's landscape / in today's world"
- "Additionally" to start a sentence
- "Let's get into it" / "Let's explore" / any variation other than "Let's dive in."
- "It's worth noting that" — just say the thing
- "Fundamentally" — cut it entirely
- "We're excited to announce" — just state the thing
- "Thought leadership," "authentic," "robust," "holistic," "leverage," "synergy," "best practices," "solutions," "disruptive"
- "See the difference?" is acceptable — Jason uses it. Keep it.

---

**Structural tells to avoid:**

*Formatting tells:*
- Over-formatting: bold lead phrases on every bullet, including overview lists and simple checklists
- Title-casing H3s and H4s — sentence case only

*Prose tells:*
- Elaborate scenario-painting in intros instead of a simple, blunt problem statement
- Withholding the core framework until the step-by-step section
- Following a strong line with 2–3 sentences elaborating why it's strong
- Assuming the reader already has a strategy or existing system in place
- **Rule of three defaults:** AI defaults to triplets — three adjectives, three phrases, three examples. If a list has exactly three items, ask whether the structure is serving the content or just filling out a pattern. If it's the latter, cut one or add a fourth.
- **Elegant variation (synonym substitution):** AI avoids repeating a word by swapping in a synonym. Real writers repeat the important word. If you see "strategy... approach... methodology" referring to the same thing, pick one word and stick with it.
- **Vague attributions:** "Experts argue," "industry reports suggest," "observers have noted," "studies show" — cite the actual source or cut the claim entirely.
- **The "Despite its... faces challenges" conclusion formula:** Endings that acknowledge strengths then pivot to challenges. Cut it. End with conviction.
- **False ranges:** "from small startups to enterprise companies" — if the range isn't meaningful or specific, cut it.

---

**Final test:**
Would the client read this out loud and feel comfortable putting their name on it? If it sounds like it could have been written by any agency or any AI, rewrite it.

| Content Type | Target Length |
|---|---|
| Hub pages | 2,500–4,000 words |
| Spoke blog posts | 2,000–3,000 words |
